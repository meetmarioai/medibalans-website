Global Constraint Rule

Why estrogen metabolism must be measured

Serum estrogen levels tell you what is circulating. Essential Estrogens tells you what happens to estrogen afterwards — how it is broken down, which metabolites are formed, and whether they protect against or increase the risk of hormone-driven conditions.

2-OH metabolites are protective. 4-OH metabolites can form DNA adducts with carcinogenic potential. 16-OH metabolites are potently estrogenic. The ratio between these determines the risk profile — and the COMT enzyme, whose function is affected by genetic variants, governs critical steps of the breakdown pathway.

GCR — Estrogen metabolism in the constraint hierarchy

COMT rs4680 (Val/Met, Met/Met) impairs detoxification of catecholestrogens (4-OH) and potentially increases risk of hormone-driven cancers. Essential Estrogens confirms whether the genetic risk is actually expressed functionally — invisible in conventional serum estrogen tests.

What It Measures

Five clinical measurement domains

Domain 01 · Estrogens

Estrogen Profile

Estrone (E1), estradiol (E2) and estriol (E3) are measured in urine via fasted morning void sample. Provides the complete estrogen profile in functional context rather than as a snapshot value in serum.

Domain 02 · Protective Metabolites

2-OH Metabolites

2-hydroxy-estrone and 2-hydroxy-estradiol are protective estrogen metabolites with weak estrogenic activity. High 2-OH production via the CYP1A1/1A2 pathway is associated with lower risk of estrogen-driven cancer.

Domain 03 · Risk Metabolites

4-OH Metabolites

4-hydroxy-estrone and 4-hydroxy-estradiol are potentially harmful metabolites that can form DNA-reactive quinone complexes. The COMT enzyme detoxifies 4-OH metabolites via methylation — impaired COMT function increases 4-OH accumulation.

Domain 04 · Estrogenic Activity

16-OH Metabolites

16-alpha-hydroxy-estrone is a potently estrogenic metabolite. Elevated 16-OH production is associated with increased proliferative signalling in estrogen-receptor-positive tissue — breast, endometrium, cervix.

Domain 05 · Methylation

Methylation Activity

2-methoxyestrone and 4-methoxyestrone are measured as indicators of COMT enzyme function. These methylated metabolites are inactive and safe. The methylated/unmethylated ratio confirms COMT activity's actual clinical expression.

FAQ

Questions about Essential Estrogens

Essential Estrogens measures the eight primary estrogen metabolites in urine — estrone (E1), estradiol (E2), estriol (E3) and their hydroxylated breakdown products via the 2-OH, 4-OH and 16-OH pathways. FMV (Fasted Morning Void) is the preferred collection method for hormone metabolites as it reflects the body's detoxification activity overnight without dietary interference.

2-OH metabolites are protective — weak estrogenic activity, efficiently methylated by COMT to inactive forms. 4-OH metabolites are potentially harmful — they can form reactive quinone adducts with DNA if COMT detoxification is impaired. 16-OH metabolites are potently estrogenic and associated with proliferative signalling in estrogen-receptor-positive tissue. Clinical guidelines indicate that the 2-OH/16-OH ratio should exceed 2.0 for optimal risk profile.

The methylation index measures the actual activity of the COMT enzyme — how efficiently catecholestrogens are methylated to inactive methoxy forms. An impaired methylation index indicates that 4-OH metabolites are not being detoxified quickly enough, increasing the risk of DNA adduct formation. COMT is magnesium-dependent — magnesium deficiency, which is common and invisible in standard tests, directly impairs COMT activity.

Essential Estrogens is indicated for familial breast cancer risk, estrogen-driven fibrocystic breast disease, endometriosis, PMS, menopausal symptoms and for known COMT or CYP1B1 variants on WGS. It is particularly valuable when serum estrogen is normal but estrogen-driven symptomatology persists — a discordance often explained by an unfavourable metabolite profile rather than absolute estrogen levels.
