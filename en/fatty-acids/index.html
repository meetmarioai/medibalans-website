Global Constraint Rule

Why fatty acid status must be measured

The fatty acid profile is one of the most underestimated markers in functional medicine. An imbalance between omega-6 and omega-3 drives systemic low-grade inflammation that sustains chronic pain, cardiovascular risk and autoimmune conditions — without appearing in standard blood work.

Fatty acids in red blood cells reflect the past 90 days of dietary intake and cell membrane status. An omega-3 index below 8% is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease. The AA/EPA ratio measures the inflammation balance at the cellular level.

GCR — The role of fatty acids in the constraint hierarchy

Elevated arachidonic acid (AA) and low EPA indicate a pro-inflammatory cellular environment that undermines all downstream protocols. The fatty acid profile determines whether omega-3 supplementation is indicated — and at what dose — before other interventions are initiated.

What It Measures

Five clinical measurement domains

Domain 01 · Omega-3

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

EPA, DHA and DPA are measured in red blood cells. The omega-3 index — the proportion of EPA+DHA of total fatty acids in the erythrocyte membrane — is an independent cardiovascular risk marker. Values below 4% are associated with significantly increased risk.

Domain 02 · Omega-6

Omega-6 & Inflammation

Arachidonic acid (AA) and linoleic acid are measured. The AA/EPA ratio is the most clinically useful inflammation index — reflecting the balance between pro- and anti-inflammatory eicosanoid pathways at the cellular level.

Domain 03 · Saturated Fatty Acids

Saturated Fatty Acids

Palmitic and stearic acids are measured. Elevated saturated fatty acids in the cell membrane affect membrane fluidity and insulin signalling. The source — dietary vs endogenous synthesis — provides clinical guidance.

Domain 04 · Trans Fats

Trans-Fatty Acids

Elaidic acid and other industrial trans fats are measured. Trans fats are incorporated into the cell membrane and disrupt normal fatty acid conversion. Clinical relevance for cardiovascular risk and inflammatory conditions.

Domain 05 · Cardiovascular Risk

Cardiovascular Risk Profile

Omega-3 index, AA/EPA ratio and total fatty acid profile are integrated into a cardiovascular risk assessment. Provides objective data for omega-3 dosing and dietary intervention.

About the Fatty Acid Analysis

The test measures the fatty acid composition in red blood cells — reflecting the past 90 days of cell membrane status. It covers omega-3 fatty acids (EPA, DHA, DPA, ALA), omega-6 fatty acids (arachidonic acid, linoleic acid, GLA), saturated fatty acids (palmitic, stearic), monounsaturated fatty acids (oleic acid), trans fatty acids (elaidic acid), and calculated risk markers including the omega-3 index and AA/EPA ratio.

The omega-3 index is the proportion of EPA+DHA of total fatty acids in the erythrocyte membrane. It is an independent cardiovascular risk marker with strong epidemiological evidence. Values below 4% are associated with significantly increased risk of sudden cardiac death. The optimal range is 8–12%. The test provides the objective data needed to dose omega-3 supplementation correctly based on actual biological effect.

The AA/EPA ratio measures the balance between arachidonic acid (pro-inflammatory omega-6) and eicosapentaenoic acid EPA (anti-inflammatory omega-3) in the cell membrane. The ratio reflects the inflammatory tone at the cellular level — more reliable than CRP for chronic low-grade inflammation. Japanese population studies show that a ratio below 2.5 is associated with the lowest cardiovascular risk.

The fatty acid analysis can be collected as a fingerstick blood sample at home or as a venous blood sample. The kit is shipped to your address with detailed instructions. The fingerstick sample is collected in the morning in a fasted state and sent directly to Genova's laboratory with the prepaid return envelope.
